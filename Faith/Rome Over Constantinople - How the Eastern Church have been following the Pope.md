
# 1. Canon 28 of Chalcedon Discourse

## 1.1. Context
: At that  time (451 AD), Constantinople  houses the emperor and the Senate

: Canon 28 of the Council of Chalcedon(451 AD) is made to elevate the status  of the Bishop of Constantinople to be higher in authority than the Bishop of Alexandria. In that council, the current pope is Pope Leo, in which he deployed papal legates (representatives) to attend and discuss in the council.

: Reply from Pope Leo:

>	Indeed resolutions of bishops which are repugnant to the rules of the holy canons composed at Nicaea, in conjunction with the loyalty of your faith, we dismiss as invalid, and by the authority of Peter, the blessed apostle, we absolutely disannul by a general decree in all ecclesiastical cases.
>	
>	St. Leo responding to Canon 28 of Chalcedon
>	
>	E. Giles, Documents Illustrating Papal Authority: A.D. 96-454 (London: S.P.C.K, 1952), 328.

: This letter shows that the pope rejects and even nullifies the canon.

: Reply from the Bishop of Constantinople

>	As for those things which the universal council of Chalcedon recently ordained in favor of the church of Constantinople, let Your Holiness be sure that there was no fault in me, who from my youth have always loved peace and quiet, keeping myself in humility. It was the most reverend clergy of the church of Constantinople who were eager about it, and they were equally supported by the most reverend priests of those parts, who agreed about it. Even so the whole force and confirmation of the acts was reserved for the authority of your blessedness. Therefore let Your Holiness know for certain that I did nothing to further the matter, having always held myself bound to avoid the lusts of pride and covetousness.
>	
>	Bishop of Constantinople in Reply to Leo

: This letter shows how the Bishop of Constantinople personally lowers himself under the Pope's Authority, acknowledging the supremacy of whatever the pope decides to be done.

## 1.2. Common Counterargument : The Bishop of Constantinople only wrote that out of etiquette

: The common counterargument by the Eastern Orthodox Church is that the Bishop of Constantinople only wrote that matter to execute what we call as *captatio benevolentiae* (gaining the goodwill of the audience).

: The bishop is replied in that way to diplomatically discourse from one official to another within the etiquette of the 5th Century.

## 1.3. Rebuttal : Ecclesiastical Intent over Imperial Purpose

: Regardless of what manner or etiquette to use, an official letter must be true to its words. 

: The rejection of the pope is made to preserve the church order of the Council of Nicaea, in which the Bishop of Alexandria and Antioch is the second and third to Rome respectively.

: Regardless of the emperor and Senate's will of making Constantinople the New Rome, the Church does not depend on any empire, nor compromise to any imperial convenience unless decided by the Pope whom the ecclesiastical (not imperial) community see him the supreme authority passed down from apostle Peter.
